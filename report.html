Computer Sceice Coursework 2022 Report

Investiagtion and plan

For my final coursework project, I was tasked with designing a smart home system. With smart home being one of the buzzwords of 2022 I was intrigued by this project. I had to create an embedded system that could be used as part of a home automation system. It had several basic requirements that needed to be met. I also had to create an interactive website that allowed users to see what the product was about. After researching many different ideas and methods of approach I originally decided to design an alarm system, but after further investigation and research I realised that was not achievable due to it not reaching the requirements I wanted it to reach. I decided to consult with my teacher about my idea and they suggested a soil moisture sensor which is the final idea I ended up going with. The soil moisture sensor had the requirements I was looking for like temperature sensor, microbits that communicate, and ThingSpeak. The requirements for my artefact are radio communication, temperature sensor, Wi-Fi connection, ThingSpeak and a pump that waters your plant automatically. The idea of my project is that it is put in a greenhouse and detects the amount of moisture in the soil of your plant and automatically waters it if it gets too dry. It also detects the temperature and alerts the other microbit inside your home if it is too hot or cold for your plant by a traffic light led system. The microbits communicate to each other through radio communication, with one also connected to Thingspeak which is continuously uploading data of the temperature to the user's profile. An automatic IOT that opens and closes windows if it gets too hot or cold is something that I should have included in my final artefact. This would have improved the system and made it completely hands free.

Design

After my researching and planning was finished, I began on the design of my artefact. I started with the basics of getting two microbits to communicate with each other through radio signal and went from there to get the artefact to meet the brief. I then got one microbit set up with the moisture sensor by using forever loops and if statements. I designed it so that it used pins and LED patterns to indicate what was happening. After I got that to work, I moved onto the next microbit that indicates when the temperature is too hot, too cold, or perfect. Uses a traffic light system that shows what is going on. It uses a ‘on radio received’ loop to carry out the actions needed. After I had all the code working, I moved onto setting up the microbit and wiring it up. Doing this I encountered many problems like wires not working properly and Wi-Fi connection being unstable. I had to watch videos and research different methods of how to properly wire the artefact which took a bit of time, but I got it working properly in the end. The Wi-Fi connection was difficult to fix as there was an unstable connection where I was setting it up. I eventually got a stable connection to be able to demonstrate my project by using my hotspot on my phone. The microbit that measures the temperature records the data to thingspeak and uploads it for the user to see. Thingspeak constantly uploads the data every minute or so and graphs the data for it to be easy to read. The other microbit sends power to the water pump to allow it to work. Each coloured wire has a different purpose, the red wire provides power to the sensor, the black wire is the grounding wire, and the yellow wire is the trigger. This is an overall efficient design that can be worked automatically with minimal user interference. The wires on the receiving microbit are colour coded so it is simple to follow and understand. The green wire provides power to the green LED. The red wire to the red LED and yellow wire to yellow LED.
